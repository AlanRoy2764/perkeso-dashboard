What the data is telling us, six findings that matter.

The aggregate numbers tell a "weaker market" story. The cross-sector pattern tells a more specific one — and it's the pattern, not the totals, that's most consistent with what an AI-led productivity shift looks like at the labour-market level.

Services collapsed.

Services accounted for ~80% of the rise in EIS claims and the entire decline in placements. Vacancies in Services fell 50,000 between 2025 and 2026 — a 20% drop in one year.

Manufacturing held the line.

The only Main Industry where vacancies grew across all three years (+29% since 2024). Placements peaked in 2025 then plateaued — physical-skill demand is decoupling from the white-collar slowdown.

Construction crashed harder than headline.

Vacancies down 42% over two years — roughly triple the all-industry decline. EIS claims doubled. This is more macro (project pipeline + funding) than AI, but it's compounding the white-collar squeeze.

The cycle is getting faster.

2024 → 2025 was a flat year. The whole shift happened in the last 12 months: +49% LOE, −25% placements, −12% vacancies. Whatever's driving this hit decisively after Q1 2025.

The vacancy-to-placement gap widened.

Vacancies fell 12%, but placements fell 25% — nearly double. Either matching is breaking down (skills mismatch) or career-fair channels are losing ground to direct AI-mediated hiring. Worth tracking.

The pattern fits the AI thesis — but isn't proof.

Services down, Manufacturing stable, fastest-shifting in 2025/2026 — that's textbook displacement of routine cognitive work. Subsidy reform, MYR weakness, and post-pandemic baselines also fit. Read it as a strong correlation, not causation.

Three years on, Malaysia's labour market is rewriting where work concentrates — and Services is the canary.

From Q1 2024 to Q1 2026, the headline numbers are unambiguous. Job losses are up 78%. Vacancies are down 13%. Placements are down 19%. What makes Q1 2026 different from any prior post-COVID quarter isn't just that all three moved in the wrong direction — it's that they moved in concert, and almost entirely through the Services sector.

The signature is unusual. In a classic demand recession, Construction and Manufacturing crater first. Here, Manufacturing actively expanded vacancies (+29% over the window) while Services — the part of the economy most exposed to generative-AI-augmented work — contracted on every measure.

That's the same shape the IMF, OECD, and ILO have flagged in their 2025 outlooks: the first labour-market measurable from AI adoption isn't mass unemployment, it's a quiet thinning of mid-skill cognitive roles showing up as fewer postings, slower hiring, and concentrated displacement in services-heavy economies. Malaysia's PERKESO data through Q1 2026 fits that template more cleanly than any other macro story we tested.

The honest read: the AI inflection isn't the only thing happening — diesel subsidy reform, ringgit pressure, and a weaker China demand pulse are all in the mix. But the cross-sector divergence is the strongest single signal, and it's getting stronger every quarter. Q2 2026 is the one to watch.

Methodology

Source
PERKESO Labour Market Exchange (LMX) public statistics — three datasets: Loss of Employment (ST/LOE/PUB/72), Job Placement (ST/PLM/PUB/71), Job Vacancy (ST/VAC/PUB/69).
Period
Q1 = January–March of each year. LOE & Placement filtered by Quarter=1. Vacancy has no quarter field; filtered by Month Rank ∈ {1,2,3}.
Scope
Malaysia-wide, all 21 MSIC industries rolled up to the 5 Main Industry groups. "Revision Stage" rows excluded from sector charts.
Caveats
Vacancy "Total" sums Active + Closed listings posted in the window — a volume measure, not net new openings. Placements reflect career-fair throughput only.
Refresh
Source dataset last refreshed 2026-03-07; row-level data covers through March 2026.
